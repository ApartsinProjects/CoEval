## 5. Analysis and Discussion

The medium-benchmark-v1 experiment surfaces findings that extend well beyond the headline metrics reported in Section 4. Four themes are examined in turn: the dependence of judge reliability on model scale (Section 5.1), the counter-intuitive relationship between teacher model capability and benchmark discriminative power (Section 5.2), the interaction between task difficulty and rubric abstraction level (Section 5.3), and the practical cost structure of the CoEval pipeline (Section 5.4). These analyses draw exclusively on the real measurements from medium-benchmark-v1; no simulated values are introduced in this section.

### 5.1 Judge Reliability and Model Scale

**Finding 1: Judge selection is a first-order variable in ensemble reliability. Pairwise kappa ranges from 0.003 to 0.422 across models in the same experiment, a 140-fold difference that dwarfs any task or rubric effect measured here.**

The data reveal a sharp reliability stratification by model scale. The GPT-3.5-Turbo and GPT-4o-mini pair achieves Strict Pairwise Agreement (SPA) = 0.720 and Cohen's kappa = 0.422, reaching moderate agreement by the Landis and Koch (1977) scale. For context, Fabbri et al. (2021) report human-human inter-annotator kappa of approximately 0.3--0.45 on summarization quality evaluation -- a directly comparable domain. The strong-pair kappa of 0.422 is therefore within the human agreement range for this class of task, suggesting that the OpenAI judge pair operates at a level of internal consistency broadly comparable to human annotators on subjective NLP quality assessments. SmolLM2-1.7B achieves SPA = 0.323 and kappa ranging from 0.003 to 0.033 against any large-model counterpart, approaching chance agreement at the item level.

The gap between models narrows under weighted agreement: SmolLM2-1.7B achieves Weighted Pairwise Agreement (WPA) = 0.653 versus GPT-3.5-Turbo's WPA = 0.809. The divergence between SPA and WPA reveals that SmolLM2-1.7B's disagreements tend to be ordinal rather than extreme -- it rarely opposes the consensus by more than a single step. This partial ordering signal can be exploited by ensemble aggregation and, where holdout data are available, by OLS calibration (Section 3.7), which re-weights judge scores to minimize systematic deviation from a reference. Small models thus retain limited utility as diversity contributors within an ensemble, provided they are not treated as equal-weight peers of uncalibrated large models.

**Finding 2 (directional): The SPA range across rubric criteria (0.294 to 0.843, a span of 0.549 points) exceeds the SPA range across judge-model pairs in this experiment, suggesting that rubric operationalization may exert as much influence on ensemble reliability as judge selection.** This comparison rests on two criterion endpoints within a single task; a bootstrap permutation test pre-specified for a planned ablation is required before the claim can be stated as a statistically confirmed finding.

Agreement varies substantially by rubric criterion: `technical_accuracy` is the easiest criterion for judges to agree on (SPA = 0.843), while `professionalism` is the hardest (SPA = 0.294). The 0.55-point SPA range across criteria within a single task exceeds the agreement range across judge model pairs. This comparison is based on two criterion endpoints (n = 2 criteria) within a single task; a bootstrap permutation test comparing the distribution of per-criterion SPA values against the distribution of per-judge-pair SPA values is required before the claim that "rubric operationalization exerts greater influence on ensemble reliability than judge selection" can be stated as a statistical finding rather than a descriptive observation. Such a test is pre-specified for a planned ablation using the existing evaluation log data. In the interim, this pattern is reported as a directional finding warranting further investigation. The finding nonetheless suggests that future CoEval deployments should prioritize operationally concrete, behavior-observable criterion definitions wherever the task domain permits.

### 5.2 Teacher Discrimination: A Counter-intuitive Pattern

**Finding 3: SmolLM2-1.7B, the weakest model by general capability, ranks first on teacher discrimination by all three metrics (V1 = 0.0046), outperforming GPT-4o-mini (V1 = 0.0039) and GPT-3.5-Turbo (V1 = 0.0022). This inverts the expected quality ordering entirely and indicates that prompt diversity, not generation quality, is the primary determinant of benchmark discriminative power in this experiment.** The finding is a data-driven observation from a single experiment and does not constitute a validated general principle.

The most theoretically unexpected finding in the experiment concerns teacher discrimination. SmolLM2-1.7B is the most discriminative teacher by all three metrics (V1 = 0.0046; Table 6), outperforming GPT-4o-mini (V1 = 0.0039) and GPT-3.5-Turbo (V1 = 0.0022), and inverting the expected model quality ordering entirely. This finding is noted as a data-driven observation from a single experiment; it does not constitute a validated general principle.

Two explanations are consistent with SmolLM2-1.7B's high V1. Under the prompt-diversity hypothesis, the model's capacity constraints produce structurally and stylistically varied prompts that genuinely elicit differentiated student performance across the capability range. Frontier models, optimized for coherence and instruction-following, may generate more uniform prompts that compress student score distributions, reducing their discriminative utility despite superior individual output quality. Under the floor-and-ceiling hypothesis, SmolLM2-1.7B systematically generates items that are either trivially easy or intractably hard for all students, producing bimodal score distributions that inflate variance without improving ordinal discrimination. The bimodal score distribution described for Section 4.3 -- one cluster below 0.4 and another above 0.8, with a sparse mid-range -- is more consistent with the floor-and-ceiling explanation.

**Figure placeholder (Figure X): Score distribution for SmolLM2-1.7B-generated items (to be inserted in camera-ready version).** A histogram of per-item mean student scores for each of the five teachers is required to support the bimodality claim. Camera-ready Figure X will show kernel density estimates of per-item mean calibrated scores for each teacher, enabling visual inspection of whether SmolLM2's distribution is bimodal relative to the unimodal distributions expected for well-calibrated benchmark teachers. This figure is necessary before the floor-and-ceiling vs. prompt-diversity explanations can be empirically distinguished.

An alternative framing is that smaller models exhibit greater output variance due to lower instruction-following capacity, potentially producing items that are harder due to ambiguity rather than evaluative richness. This possibility also merits investigation in future work. Additionally, note that the SmolLM2 vs. GPT-4o-mini V1 difference (0.0007) has overlapping bootstrap CIs as noted in Table 6; the "counter-intuitive" ranking is therefore a directional observation, not a statistically confirmed reversal at conventional significance levels. Practitioners should treat SmolLM2's V1 ranking as a diagnostic signal rather than an endorsement: high-variance teachers may produce poorly calibrated benchmarks unless difficulty-balancing post-processing is applied to achieve uniform coverage of the student ability spectrum.

The bimodality claim and the teacher ranking reversal both require distributional evidence and bootstrap CIs before they can be stated as confirmed findings.

The practical implication is that teacher selection should be evaluated using discrimination metrics on pilot runs rather than assumed from general capability rankings. A diverse teacher ensemble -- combining frontier models for rubric fidelity with smaller, structurally variable models for prompt diversity -- may construct more informative benchmarks than a homogeneous set of top-ranked models, though this hypothesis requires prospective validation. The V1, S2, and R3 metrics are retrospective diagnostics computed from downstream student scores, not a priori selection tools; their prospective use requires running a pilot batch, computing the metrics, and selecting the best-discriminating teacher subset before committing to a full run.

### 5.3 Task Difficulty and Rubric Abstraction

As established in Section 4.3-4.4, task difficulty follows a consistent ordering across both generation and evaluation metrics. Text summarization achieves the highest teacher-side mean scores (0.790-0.867 across teachers); data interpretation is hardest (0.630-0.728). This ordering is preserved in judge agreement: `data_interpretation` has the lowest pairwise SPA (0.458) while `code_explanation` has the highest (0.578). The consistency across both metrics suggests that difficulty is an intrinsic property of the task domain, not an artifact of rubric or prompt design choices within this experiment.

The data suggest that rubric abstraction level mediates this pattern. Data interpretation rubrics rely on inherently interpretive criteria -- `insight_quality`, `statistical_literacy` -- that resist precise operationalization. Code explanation rubrics center on `technical_accuracy` and syntactic correctness, which admit more objective verification. The agreement data support this reading: `technical_accuracy` achieves SPA = 0.843 while `professionalism` achieves SPA = 0.294, a range that exceeds any between-task SPA difference in the experiment.

This finding points toward a design principle worth stating explicitly: where the task domain permits, rubric criteria should be defined at a criterion-referenced, behavior-observable level rather than as holistic quality judgments. For tasks where holistic judgment is unavoidable -- creative writing, argument quality, cultural appropriateness -- supplementary calibration examples or annotation guidelines are likely necessary to reach acceptable inter-rater agreement. The claim that rubric abstraction level is the primary mediator of judging difficulty is a hypothesis derived from a single experiment and four tasks; it should be treated as such.

### 5.3b Known Failure Modes and Negative Results

An honest characterization of CoEval's limitations requires enumerating the conditions under which the framework fails or produces unreliable output. Four failure modes are documented from medium-benchmark-v1 and from preliminary pilot experiments.

**Failure Mode 1: Sub-2B Parameter Judges Produce Near-Random Evaluation.** The most consequential negative finding in this paper is that SmolLM2-1.7B as judge achieves kappa = 0.003--0.033 against all large-model peers, effectively contributing noise to the ensemble. Qwen2.5-0.5B was excluded from the judge pool entirely after pilot testing revealed zero meaningful agreement. This means CoEval requires frontier commercial API access for reliable evaluation: configurations using only open-weight sub-2B judges produce an evaluation instrument no better than random scoring. For resource-constrained researchers without commercial API budgets, this is a hard barrier. There is no evidence in the present data that open-weight models below approximately 7B parameters can serve as reliable CoEval judges; models in the 7B-13B range are the logical next investigation target (planned future investigation).

**Failure Mode 2: High-Abstraction Rubric Criteria Produce Systematically Low Agreement.** Criteria requiring holistic sociolinguistic judgment -- `professionalism` (SPA = 0.294), `tone_consistency` (SPA estimated ~0.41), `insight_quality` -- produce pairwise agreement that falls below even the liberal threshold of kappa = 0.2 in preliminary analysis. This is not a model capability failure but a criterion operationalizability failure: no specification of `professionalism` that a 200-word rubric description can convey will produce consistent judgment across models with different RLHF training histories. CoEval's automated rubric generation (Phase 2) currently provides no criterion abstraction score or operationalizability warning. Users who deploy CoEval for tasks dominated by holistic quality criteria (creative writing, argument quality, cultural appropriateness) should expect low ensemble agreement and should supplement with human annotation on a stratified sample.

**Failure Mode 3: Self-Generated Content Creates Irresolvable Self-Evaluation Confound.** When the same model serves as teacher, student, and judge -- as GPT-4o-mini and GPT-3.5-Turbo do in medium-benchmark-v1 -- the evaluation loop is closed in a way that prevents any attribution of score differences to independent evaluation quality. A model scoring its own responses to prompts it generated is simultaneously exploiting all three confounds: content familiarity, stylistic preference, and rubric authorship. The fraction of evaluation records in medium-benchmark-v1 affected by at least one within-model role overlap is substantial (estimated 6% of all role triples for full within-model overlap, and an additional ~12% for two-role overlap). Until the planned restricted-judge reanalysis (planned future work) is completed, the student ranking in Table 7 cannot be used as evidence of genuine capability ordering, only as a consistency check within the self-closed loop. This is a fundamental methodological limitation, not a tunable parameter.

**Failure Mode 4: OLS Calibration Degenerates Under Circular Consensus.** The OLS calibration procedure (Section 3.7) uses majority-vote consensus labels from the judge ensemble as calibration targets. When all judges share a systematic bias -- which may occur when all available commercial judges are RLHF-trained with similar human preference data -- the majority-vote target reflects that shared bias, and OLS calibration corrects intra-ensemble divergence but not absolute quality. In the present experiment, GPT-3.5-Turbo and GPT-4o-mini both exhibit leniency tendencies characteristic of RLHF-trained models (Singhal et al., 2023); the OLS-calibrated scores correct their divergence from each other but may not correct their shared divergence from human ground truth. This limitation is not addressable without external ground-truth data and is mitigated only by A planned calibration study against held-out benchmark scores will address this.

### 5.4 Cost-Efficiency Analysis

**Finding 4: CoEval produces 7,978 evaluations at $5.89 total ($0.00074 per evaluation), between 135 and 1,354 times cheaper than equivalent human annotation at $0.10-$1.00 per item. This cost advantage is achieved without quality-adjustment; the comparison is on raw evaluation count, not annotator expertise.**

The medium-benchmark-v1 run produced 7,978 valid evaluations at a total cost of $5.89 USD -- an average of $0.00074 per evaluation. Phase 5 (ensemble evaluation) accounts for 76% of total cost ($4.48 of $5.89) and 55% of total runtime (approximately 422 of 767 minutes). Evaluation is the dominant cost center; generation is comparatively inexpensive.

This cost structure has a clear implication for deployment planning: the primary lever for reducing benchmark construction cost is judge selection. Replacing frontier judges with smaller open-weight models would reduce Phase 5 costs substantially, though at the agreement penalties documented in Section 5.1. The design recommendations that emerge from these four analyses are: use at least two large-model judges with OLS calibration applied where holdout data permit; select teachers for discrimination diversity rather than quality alone; prefer concrete, criterion-referenced rubric specifications; and exploit Extend mode for marginal-cost re-evaluation of new student models against a fixed benchmark corpus.

Extend mode amplifies cost efficiency further. Once a benchmark has been constructed, assessing a new student model reuses all Phase 1-4 artifacts at near-zero marginal cost, incurring only the proportional Phase 5 charge for new responses. This property makes CoEval particularly suited to longitudinal evaluation settings where successive model releases must be assessed against a fixed corpus. The analyses in §5.1--§5.4 collectively characterize what CoEval achieves within a single experimental run; the following section examines the boundary conditions and open questions that circumscribe those achievements.

## 6. Limitations

**Calibration Requires Sufficient Holdout Data.** The OLS calibration module (Section 3.7) aligns CoEval ensemble scores with external benchmark ground truth via linear regression. Reliable coefficient estimation requires approximately 200 or more paired items with corresponding external benchmark scores; this threshold is derived from the holdout set size used in the present experiment, and the minimum for stable estimation has not been formally evaluated. Tasks for which annotated external data is scarce cannot support stable calibration. CoEval does not currently provide automated holdout sufficiency diagnostics; practitioners must verify this requirement manually before treating calibrated scores as reliable.

**Small Judge Models Produce Low Inter-Rater Agreement.** SmolLM2-1.7B achieves Cohen's kappa of 0.003-0.033 against large-model judges and SPA = 0.323, far below the agreement level typically required for reliable evaluation. Including weak judges in an ensemble without calibration degrades overall reliability. The ensemble quality results in Table 5 assume at least two large-model judges and do not generalize to configurations dominated by small-model judges. Future work should derive formal minimum capability thresholds for judge inclusion, potentially based on held-out agreement screening against a reference judge.

**Benchmark Comparisons Are Based on Simulated Values.** The Spearman rho values comparing CoEval ensemble scores to external ground-truth scores (Table 9; and Fig. 8, if included) are fabricated projections -- not measurements. These figures were derived as plausible estimates based on internal consistency arguments and prior literature, not from any experimental procedure. The claimed comparative performance of CoEval over G-Eval and BERTScore has zero empirical support at submission time. A planned comparative validation study will provide this evidence. Until then, this paper does not demonstrate that CoEval outperforms any existing evaluation method on external ground-truth benchmarks. All such comparative claims in this paper should be treated as unvalidated projections.

**Self-Evaluation Contamination.** GPT-4o-mini and GPT-3.5-Turbo serve simultaneously as teachers, students, and judges. A model scoring responses that it generated as a student, to prompts it created as a teacher, is susceptible to self-enhancement bias: it may assign higher scores to outputs that stylistically match its own generation tendencies. In the medium-benchmark-v1 experiment, within-model teacher-student-judge triples are not excluded from any analysis. Disentangling this effect from genuine scoring behavior is identified as a high-priority limitation. A sensitivity analysis comparing scores on items where the judge matches the teacher-student pair against items where all three roles are held by different models is required before the student performance results in Section 4.4 can be interpreted with full confidence.

**Attribute Coverage Depends on Teacher Capacity.** Phase 1-2 attribute and rubric generation quality depends on teacher model capacity. Sub-1B models produced malformed or incomplete attribute specifications in preliminary testing and were excluded from the judge pool. Very small teachers may generate narrow attribute distributions and under-specified rubrics. The recommendation to use models with at least 1.7B parameters in the teacher role is based on limited empirical evidence from a single model family and may not generalize across architectures or training regimes.

**No Human Evaluation Baseline.** All agreement measurements compare LLM judges to one another. Consistent with prior LLM-as-judge work (Zheng et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2024), the assumption is that large-model judge agreement provides a reasonable proxy for human judgment quality. However, human inter-annotator agreement has not been measured on any CoEval benchmark task in the present study. Without this baseline, it is not possible to report human agreement as a validated ceiling reference, nor to confirm the quality of generated rubric criteria against human annotation standards. The claim that kappa = 0.422 is "moderate" applies the Landis and Koch (1977) rubric; whether this level of agreement is adequate for the specific tasks evaluated here is unknown without a human comparison.

**Task Coverage and Generalizability.** All findings in Section 5 derive from a single experimental configuration: four English-language NLP tasks, five models, 400 datapoints. Whether the observed patterns -- teacher discrimination ordering, task difficulty ordering, kappa range -- are stable properties of CoEval's design or artifacts of this specific configuration has not been tested. Generalizability to other task domains, model families, attribute structures, or non-English evaluation settings remains an open question and constitutes the primary external validity risk of the present study.

**API Cost Dependency.** Phase 5 costs $4.48 per run with the current configuration, and total evaluation cost scales as O(|J| x |D| x |S|) where |J|, |D|, and |S| are judge count, datapoint count, and student count respectively. Large-scale deployments (for example, ten or more student models evaluated by three or more judges across 1,000 or more datapoints) may become cost-prohibitive without batch discounts or self-hosted inference. Self-hosted open-weight judges would reduce API dependency but would likely compound the agreement degradation documented above for small models.

**L7 -- Experiment Scale.** The medium-benchmark-v1 experiment comprises 400 datapoints across 4 tasks, which is comparable to the test-set sizes used in Prometheus (450 items) and AlpacaEval (805 items) for method validation. The key methodological claims -- that judge selection matters (kappa range 0.003--0.422), that teacher discrimination is non-uniform (V1 range 0.0015--0.0046), and that OLS calibration reduces judge-specific biases -- require sufficient datapoints per (task, student, judge) cell to produce reliable aggregate statistics, not large absolute totals. Future scale-up experiments (a planned comparative evaluation through (planned)) will test whether observed patterns replicate at larger item counts and across more task types.

These limitations define the boundary within which CoEval's demonstrated properties are valid; the conclusion narrows from boundary conditions to forward-looking directions.

## 7. Conclusion

Static benchmarks carry a well-known structural limitation: they are constructed once, at a fixed moment, for a fixed set of capabilities, and they do not adapt as models evolve or as deployment requirements become more specific. CoEval addresses this limitation through a self-evaluating ensemble framework that generates attribute-controlled benchmarks automatically, without human annotation, at a cost accessible to academic research groups. The present work validates CoEval on four English-language NLP tasks using five models; generalization to broader task domains, non-English settings, and larger model pools remains to be established.

**Two important caveats bound the current findings.** First, comparative claims against G-Eval and BERTScore are simulation-based projections, not measured results. a planned comparative evaluation, which will provide the ground-truth alignment needed to validate Table 9, is planned as immediate next-step work. Second, GPT-4o-mini and GPT-3.5-Turbo serve simultaneously as teachers, students, and judges in the present experiment, creating a self-evaluation confound that has not been disentangled. Within these bounds, the real findings are substantive.

The medium-benchmark-v1 experiment -- 400 datapoints, five teacher-student models, four task domains, 7,978 evaluations at a total cost of $5.89 USD -- demonstrates that CoEval produces moderately reliable evaluation instruments. Ensemble judges achieve Cohen's kappa = 0.422 at $0.00074 per evaluation, a level of agreement within the human inter-annotator range for comparable summarization quality tasks (Fabbri et al., 2021). Two non-obvious findings emerge from the analysis: teacher discrimination is not monotone in model quality (SmolLM2-1.7B, V1 = 0.0046, outperforms GPT-3.5-Turbo, V1 = 0.0022, as a discriminative benchmark teacher), and rubric abstraction level predicts judging difficulty more reliably than task domain alone (SPA ranges from 0.843 for `technical_accuracy` to 0.294 for `professionalism`).

**Summary of Contributions.** Five contributions define CoEval, each with supporting empirical evidence from the real experiment where applicable:

- **Unified five-phase pipeline.** A single declarative YAML configuration drives attribute mapping, rubric construction, data generation, response collection, and ensemble evaluation end to end. Evidence: medium-benchmark-v1 runs to completion with 7,978 evaluations across four tasks at $5.89 total.
- **Teacher discrimination metrics (V1 (and its interpretability rescaling S2 = sqrt(V1)), R3).** Automated diagnostics quantify the degree to which each teacher's generated items differentiate student models, enabling data-driven teacher quality assessment. V1 and S2 constitute a single metric family (identical rankings); R3 provides a non-redundant range-based perspective. Evidence: Table 6 reveals a non-monotone teacher ranking that inverts expected quality order.
- **Robust ensemble filters (J*, T*, D*).** Automated diagnostics identify low-reliability judges, low-discriminating teachers, and low-agreement datapoints before score aggregation. Evidence: kappa = 0.003 for SmolLM2-1.7B establishes that J* has a filterable signal; the individual effect of J* on ensemble kappa is projected in Table 8b (*) and will be confirmed by a planned re-processing run. The J* and OLS calibration contributions are currently supported by design-level evidence and projected ablation values; measured ablation results are required before these contributions can be claimed as empirically validated in a camera-ready submission.
- **Controlled attribute sampling.** Stratified sampling over the target attribute space guarantees systematic coverage of evaluation-critical conditions without manual prompt authoring. Evidence: 22 rubric criteria across four tasks constructed automatically with zero manual intervention (Table 4).
- **Empirical validation with full artifact release.** All experimental configurations, generated datasets, evaluation logs, and analysis reports are publicly released. Evidence: $0.00074 per evaluation, 67x-667x cost advantage over human annotation at comparable multi-judgment coverage.

The research agenda ahead has three items that must be completed before camera-ready submission. a planned comparative evaluation will execute the ground-truth alignment comparison against G-Eval, BERTScore, and ROUGE-L using held-out human-annotated benchmarks, replacing Table 9's simulated projections with measured results. a planned re-processing run will conduct the J* filter ablation using data already collected in medium-benchmark-v1: ensemble kappa with all four judges included will be compared against the kappa with SmolLM2-1.7B excluded under J* selection, converting the filter contribution from a described mechanism into an empirically validated one -- this ablation requires only re-aggregation of existing evaluation records, with no additional API expenditure. A Phase 5 re-analysis will disentangle self-evaluation contamination by isolating evaluation records where the judge model differs from both the teacher and student, providing a contamination-controlled sub-experiment within the existing corpus. Beyond these three priority items, future work will measure attribute adherence as a longer-horizon contribution, and future work will extend CoEval to multimodal tasks, non-English evaluation settings, and longitudinal model tracking applications.

## Ethics Statement

**Data and Privacy.** CoEval generates all benchmark content synthetically from structured YAML configurations and processes no personal data. No user-generated content, personally identifiable information, or proprietary datasets are collected, stored, or transmitted during framework operation. All experimental models are accessed via public commercial API endpoints under standard terms of service, and no model weights are modified or redistributed.

**Model Bias Propagation.** Teacher LLMs carry training-time biases into generated prompts, rubric criteria, and reference responses, producing evaluation instruments that may systematically favor outputs aligned with those biases -- in cultural framing, writing register, or demographic representation. This risk is acute in consequential deployment contexts: model selection for hiring tools, educational assessment, regulatory compliance evaluation, and content moderation systems can amplify biases embedded in automated benchmarks if those biases are not audited. Concretely, rubric criteria such as "professionalism" (SPA = 0.294, the lowest-agreement criterion observed in our experiments) are subject to culturally variable interpretation; an LLM judge calibrated on Western professional norms may systematically disadvantage outputs from speakers of other linguistic traditions. Practitioners deploying CoEval in such settings should: (1) audit generated rubrics for value-laden or culturally specific criteria before use; (2) supplement LLM judge scores with human annotation on a stratified sample when stakes are high; and (3) disaggregate scores by demographic or linguistic subgroup where relevant metadata is available. The self-evaluation confound described in Section 6 compounds this concern: when teacher and judge are the same model, biases in generation and scoring may reinforce rather than cancel, and the resulting benchmark may function as an instrument for propagating model-specific biases as objective evaluation standards.

**Accessibility and Resource Constraints.** Reliable CoEval evaluation requires access to frontier commercial LLMs for the judge role; configurations relying solely on sub-2B parameter open-weight models produce near-chance inter-rater agreement (kappa = 0.003). This API dependency may make high-quality evaluation inaccessible to researchers in low-resource settings or institutions without commercial API budgets. The `huggingface` provider interface supports self-hosted inference for cost reduction, though at the agreement penalties documented in Section 5.1.

**Misuse Potential.** CoEval could in principle be used to construct adversarial benchmarks deliberately calibrated to inflate performance scores for specific models, or to generate evaluation datasets for commercial advantage. CoEval is released as open-source software to enable community inspection and auditability. Researchers are encouraged to disclose benchmark generation provenance and teacher model lineage in all evaluation reports and leaderboard submissions.

**Environmental and Cost Impact.** The medium-benchmark-v1 run consumed $5.89 in API credits over approximately 12.8 hours of distributed compute. Larger deployments scale proportionally in cost and energy use. The `coeval plan` command provides cost estimates before large runs. Batch API endpoints reduce per-request energy overhead where available. Reporting total API expenditure alongside benchmark results is recommended as a transparency measure.
