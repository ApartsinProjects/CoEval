## FINAL RELATED WORK

**2. Related Work**

**2.1 LLM Evaluation Benchmarks**

Constructing evaluation benchmarks for large language models has historically demanded sustained human expertise across three interdependent phases: item authoring, quality verification, and scoring protocol design. BIG-Bench \citep{Srivastava2022} mobilized over 450 tasks contributed by more than 200 researchers across approximately two years of coordinated effort; HELM \citep{Liang2023} standardized evaluation across 42 models on 16 core scenarios but required months of engineering to instrument each new model-scenario combination. Task-specific benchmarks including MMLU \citep{Hendrycks2021}, HellaSwag \citep{Zellers2019}, TriviaQA \citep{Joshi2017}, and GSM8K \citep{Cobbe2021} achieve high discriminative validity within their respective domains but provide no mechanism for extension when practitioners require coverage of deployment-specific scenarios not anticipated at construction time. Static item pools are additionally vulnerable to memorization artifacts as training corpora expand to subsume benchmark content. LiveBench \citep{White2024} addresses contamination through continuous item refresh sourced from mathematics competitions and recent arXiv preprints, though this approach depends on externally curated refresh cycles rather than on-demand generation from user-specified task descriptions. The cost and rigidity of human-curated benchmarks motivate a scalable generation alternative, which in turn demands principled quality control. CoEval addresses the generation bottleneck directly: its teacher pipeline generates attribute-stratified items on demand from a declarative task specification, producing fresh, non-overlapping benchmark items for any deployment context without annotation campaigns.

**2.2 LLM-as-Judge Approaches**

The prohibitive cost of human annotation at benchmark scale has driven adoption of large language models as automated evaluators, yet this approach introduces its own reliability challenges. Reference-based NLG evaluation metrics -- ROUGE \citep{Lin2004} (n-gram recall against reference summaries) and BERTScore \citep{Zhang2020bertscore} (contextual embedding similarity) -- provide scalable evaluation without human annotation, but their correlation with human quality judgments is limited for tasks requiring semantic coherence or nuanced reasoning (Fabbri et al., 2021). G-Eval \citep{Liu2023geval} demonstrated that GPT-4 prompted with explicit evaluation criteria achieves near-human correlation on NLG quality dimensions including coherence and fluency, substantially outperforming ROUGE and BERTScore on open-ended generation tasks. MT-Bench \citep{Zheng2023} systematized pairwise LLM judgment for multi-turn instruction-following, reporting Spearman correlations near 0.80-0.85 between GPT-4 judgments and human preferences. PandaLM \citep{Wang2023pandalm} fine-tuned a 7B judge on human preference data to reduce API dependence. Auto-J \citep{Li2023autoj} trained a 13B open-source generative judge covering 58 real-world scenarios, demonstrating that open-weight models can match proprietary judges on diverse evaluation tasks; however, like all single-judge systems, Auto-J provides no inter-judge agreement diagnostics. AlpacaEval \citep{Dubois2024} introduced length-controlled regression to debias win-rate comparisons, substantially improving correlation with Chatbot Arena human preferences. ChatEval \citep{Chan2023} introduced multi-agent debate among multiple instances of the same base model; because same-model instances share identical weights, their judgment errors are correlated rather than independent, limiting bias cancellation.

FLAMe \citep{Vu2024flame} trained a single judge on over one hundred heterogeneous human feedback datasets, achieving strong cross-task generalization at substantial training cost. Verga et al. \citep{Verga2024} proposed replacing a single large judge with a panel of diverse, smaller models, reporting that panel agreement correlates better with human preferences than individual judges on open-ended generation; their approach, however, employs a fixed-composition panel without per-judge quality filtering or calibration against external reference scores. OffsetBias \citep{Park2024offsetbias} systematically characterized six surface-level judgment artifacts that distort scores independently of semantic quality. These findings collectively motivate calibrated ensemble judgment: CoEval deploys a heterogeneous multi-model ensemble whose Strict Pairwise Agreement (SPA) and Cohen's kappa are monitored continuously, enabling dynamic outlier discounting without fine-tuning (Table 3). Unlike fixed-panel approaches, CoEval introduces J* filtering to exclude low-agreement judges and applies OLS-calibrated ensemble scoring to correct per-judge biases.

**2.3 Synthetic Benchmark Construction**

Scalable generation of instruction data using LLMs was pioneered by Self-Instruct \citep{Wang2023selfinstruct}, which bootstrapped 52,000 tasks from 175 seed examples using GPT-3, and by WizardLM's Evol-Instruct \citep{Xu2023wizardlm}, which extended generation through iterative breadth and depth mutations to increase task complexity. Chung et al. \citep{Chung2023} studied diversity-accuracy trade-offs in LLM-generated text data, proposing logit suppression and human-in-the-loop label correction. WildBench \citep{Lin2024wildbench} curates 1,024 challenging tasks from real user-chatbot logs, achieving near-perfect Pearson correlation with Chatbot Arena Elo rankings; because it draws from observed user interactions rather than from attribute-controlled generation, however, it cannot be adapted to novel deployment scenarios without collecting new interaction data. ARES \citep{SaadFalcon2024} generates synthetic benchmark questions from a domain corpus, scores responses with an LLM judge, and calibrates judge predictions against a small set of human annotations via prediction-powered inference, demonstrating that calibrated synthetic evaluation is achievable without large labeled datasets. ARES is the most closely related prior system to CoEval on the calibration and synthetic generation dimensions.

The key architectural differences between ARES and CoEval are as follows: (1) ARES is designed specifically for retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipeline evaluation and does not generalize to arbitrary NLP task types; (2) ARES's calibration mechanism (prediction-powered inference) requires a minimum set of independently human-annotated items and does not support majority-vote consensus calibration for settings where no external labels exist; (3) ARES uses a single LLM judge, providing no inter-judge agreement diagnostics or ensemble quality filtering; and (4) ARES does not support user-specified attribute stratification axes or multi-task rubric construction. These works established that LLM-synthesized content can serve as effective evaluation material, but each addresses a specific, constrained use case: Self-Instruct and WizardLM target training data production rather than evaluation benchmark construction; WildBench requires an existing user interaction corpus; and ARES is constrained to RAG evaluation pipelines and single-judge scoring. CoEval provides all of the components missing from these prior systems for general-purpose deployment evaluation: task-level attribute axes, teacher-generated rubrics, a multi-judge evaluation loop with inter-judge diagnostics, and calibrated ensemble aggregation across arbitrary task types, producing auditable, versioned benchmarks rather than uncurated instruction corpora (Figure 3 demonstrates attribute coverage properties). A direct quantitative comparison between ARES and CoEval on shared tasks and ground-truth benchmarks will be included in a planned comparative evaluation.

**2.4 Rubric-Based and Structured Evaluation**

Rubric-grounded evaluation frameworks reduce holistic judgment subjectivity by decomposing quality into named criteria with explicit scoring anchors, but existing implementations rely on statically defined rubrics that require human authoring or domain expertise to construct. Prometheus \citep{Kim2023prometheus} fine-tuned a 13B judge on human-written rubrics, demonstrating that criterion conditioning significantly reduces score variance relative to holistic prompting. Prometheus-2 \citep{Kim2024prometheus2} extended this to support both direct assessment and pairwise ranking via weight merging across Mistral-7B and Mixtral-8x7B backbones. Both Prometheus and Prometheus-2 expose rubric-conditioned evaluation to end users but require those rubrics as input at inference time; CoEval's Phase 2 generates rubrics from the user's task description and attribute axes, producing versioned rubric artifacts as a first-class pipeline output that can be inspected, modified, and reproduced. FLASK \citep{Ye2023flask} defined a twelve-dimension rubric spanning logical correctness, readability, and factual faithfulness, and showed that rubric-disaggregated scores correlate more strongly with human preferences than holistic judgments.

FLAMe \citep{Vu2024flame} trained a multitask autorater on over five million human judgments, achieving state-of-the-art performance across twelve autorater benchmarks, but requires the full multitask training regime and does not provide on-demand rubric generation for new task specifications. SummEval \citep{Fabbri2021} benchmarked automatic summarization metrics against human judgments across four quality dimensions, demonstrating that no single metric dominates across evaluation axes. CheckList \citep{ribeiro2020beyond} decomposed NLP model capabilities into behavioral test types using manually authored templates. CoEval closes the rubric authoring bottleneck: rubrics are generated by a teacher LLM conditioned on the task definition and attribute axes, then calibrated against observed score distributions across the judge ensemble.

**2.5 Inter-Rater Agreement and Calibration**

Measurement reliability in evaluation systems rests on agreement frameworks developed across statistics, psychometrics, and NLP. Cohen's kappa \citep{Cohen1960} quantifies chance-corrected agreement for categorical ratings; Krippendorff's alpha \citep{Krippendorff2011} generalizes to ordinal and interval scales with missing observations. Snow et al. \citep{Snow2008} demonstrated that aggregating five non-expert annotations matches single expert labels on sentiment, word sense disambiguation, and textual entailment, establishing that ensemble annotation can substitute for expert annotation when annotator reliability is calibrated. WMT shared tasks \citep{Freitag2021} institutionalized inter-rater agreement monitoring in translation quality evaluation, showing that assessments with high inter-annotator kappa are more reliable predictors of downstream system ranking than holistic scores. Dawid and Skene \citep{Dawid1979} proposed an expectation-maximization model for jointly estimating latent annotator reliability and true item quality from noisy crowdsourced ratings. SummEval \citep{Fabbri2021} and OffsetBias \citep{Park2024offsetbias} both demonstrate that reliability varies substantially across judges and evaluation dimensions, underscoring the need for continuous monitoring.

Despite this theoretical apparatus, existing LLM evaluation frameworks treat agreement statistics as post-hoc diagnostics computed after scoring is complete, rather than as active signals during evaluation. CoEval operationalizes agreement as a first-class scoring mechanism: Strict Pairwise Agreement (SPA, defined in §3) and Cohen's kappa are computed incrementally during ensemble evaluation; judges falling below agreement thresholds are dynamically down-weighted following the Dawid-Skene reliability weighting principle; and the ensemble expands when minimum agreement is not reached, integrating calibrated reliability directly into the scoring pipeline rather than reporting it as an afterthought. The five gaps collectively identified across §§2.1-2.5 -- extensibility, bias calibration, attribute stratification, rubric authoring, and active agreement monitoring -- motivate the unified pipeline design described in §3.
